



Chinese ambassadors to the U.S. 
1. Chai Zemin, 1979 to 1982 
Chai was the People's Republic of China's first ambassador to the U.S. after the normalization of the relations in 1979. 
He was initially director of the Liaison Office in Washington, D.C., and was involved in the negotiations to normalize ties, which began in the summer of 1978. It was decided that year that diplomatic relations and embassies would be officially established on Jan. 1, 1979. 
Chai thus became the first ambassador to the U.S., 30 years after the founding of the PRC. 
2. Zhang Wenjin, 1983 to 1985 
Zhang was vice minister of foreign affairs before he was appointed ambassador. He had participated in many important tasks, including talks to normalize relations with the U.S. He took part in Henry Kissinger's secret talks with Premier Zhou Enlai in 1971, accompanied Zhou when he met with President Richard Nixon in 1972. 
3. Han Xu, 1985 to 1989 
Before serving as ambassador, Han Xu was also the vice minister of foreign affairs. In the 1970s and '80s, he took part in historical events to advance China-U.S. relations, such as Kissinger's visit to China in 1971 and Nixon's visit in 1973. 
He has also made an important contribution in the latter stages of negotiations on the China-US Joint Communique on Aug. 17, 1982. 
4. Zhu Qizhen, 1989 to 1993 
Like his predecessors, Zhu was vice minister of foreign affairs from 1984 to 1989, having joined the ministry in 1949. He also worked at the Chinese embassy in Egypt in the early '70s, and in 1979 he accompanied Deng Xiaoping on his historic visit to the U.S. 
5. Li Daoyu, 1993 to 1998 
Li began working for the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in 1952 and later headed its Department of International Organizations and Conferences. He became deputy delegate to Geneva in 1983, and from 1990 to 1993 was the delegate and ambassador to the U.N. 
6. Li Zhaoxing, 1998 to 2001 
Li graduated from Peking University and joined the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in 1964. He was appointed to positions in Africa during the '70s and also accompanied President Nixon on a visit to China in October 1989. He became vice minister of foreign affairs in 1995. 
During his three years in the U.S. as ambassador, China-U.S. relations advanced greatly. As a diplomat, Li was widely known for his pragmatic style and his words of wisdom at diplomatic occasions. 
He went on to serve as Minister of Foreign Affairs from 2003 to 2008. 
7. Yang Jiechi, 2001 to 2005 
Yang is a lifelong diplomat. He served as second secretary at the embassy in Washington D.C. in 1983 before becoming ambassador at the age of 51, the youngest to ever hold the role. While ambassador, he made a great contribution to ease tensions between the two countries, such as when Chinese and U.S. aircraft collided in 2001, when he made continuous efforts to expound China's position on the issue. 
Between April 2007 and 2013, Yang served as China's 10th foreign minister. In March 2013, he was elected as a state councilor. 
8. Zhou Wenzhong, 2005 to 2010 
Zhou held several high-ranking posts with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, including as deputy director of the American Affairs Department until 1994. In 2001, he was appointed vice minister of foreign affairs. During his time in the U.S. he visited more than 100 senators and members of the House of Representatives. 
9. Zhang Yesui, 2010 to 2013 
Zhang, the current vice-minister of foreign affairs, joined the ministry in the '70s. He served as China's permanent representative to the U.N. from 2008 to 2010, assuming the one-month rotating presidency of the UN Security Council in 2008. 
10. Cui Tiankai, 2013-present 
Born in 1952 in eastern Zhejiang province, Cui is a graduate of Johns Hopkins University School of Advanced International Studies. He worked for the Chinese delegation to the U.N. as an interpreter before joining the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in 1984. After serving as ambassador to Japan from 2007 to 2009, Cui was appointed vice-minister of foreign affairs. 
Sources: Chinese embassy in the US., Xinhua 
U.S. ambassadors to China 1. Leonard Freel Woodcock, 1979 to 1981 
Woodcock was president of the United Automobile Workers from 1970 until 1977, when he retired and was named head of the U.S. liaison mission in China by President Jimmy Carter. He played a pivotal role in negotiating the establishment of full diplomatic relations between the countries during the Carter administration and helped open the U.S. embassy in Beijing in 1979. He died in January 2001. 
2. Arthur W. Hummel, Jr., 1981 to 1985 
Hummel was born in China to American missionary parents. After earning a master's degree in Chinese studies at the University of Chicago, he joined the U.S. State Department in 1950 and went on to serve as assistant secretary of state for East Asian and Pacific affairs and held ambassadorial positions in Burma, Ethiopia, and Pakistan. Hummel was chief negotiator for the U.S.-China Joint Communique on U.S. Arms Sales to Taiwan in 1982, under which the U.S. stated its intention to reduce arms sales to Taiwan gradually and reaffirmed its view that "'there is but one China." After his retirement, Hummel served as chairman of an advisory council at the Johns Hopkins University-Nanjing University Center for Chinese and American Studies, in Washington D.C. He died in February 2001. 
3. Winston Lord, 1985 to 1989 
The current co-chairman of the International Rescue Committee, Lord was assistant secretary of state for East Asian and Pacific affairs from 1993 to 1997 under President Bill Clinton. Before that, he was chairman of the National Endowment for Democracy, vice chairman of the International Rescue Committee, chairman of the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace's National Commission on America and the New World, and president of the Council on Foreign Relations. 
4. James R. Lilley, 1989 to 1991 
Born in Qingdao, China, Lilley was director of the American Institute in Taiwan from 1981 to 1984 and was appointed deputy assistant secretary of state for East Asian affairs in 1985. In 2004, he published his memoir, China Hands: Nine Decades of Adventure, Espionage and Diplomacy in Asia. 
5. J. Stapleton Roy, 1991 to 1995 
Roy was born in Nanjing to American missionaries. He retired from the foreign service in January 2001 after serving the State Department for 45 years. He spent much of his career in East Asia. In 1996, he was promoted to career ambassador, the highest rank in the foreign service. 
6. James Ralph Sasser, 1995 to 1999 
Sasser was an attorney with the Nashville law firm Goodpasture, Carpenter, Woods, and Sasser from 1961 to 1977. He served from 1977 to 1995 in the Senate, and then became a fellow of the Harvard University Kennedy School of Government in 1995. 
7. Joseph W. Prueher, 1999 to 2001 
Before assuming his duties in China, Prueher completed 35 years of service in the U.S. Navy in May 1999 and was commander-in-chief of the Pacific command from 1996 until his retirement. 
8. Clark T. Randt, Jr., 2001 to 2009 
Randt is the longest-serving U.S. ambassador to China. He was a resident of Beijing from 1982 until 1984, serving as the embassy's first secretary and commercial attache. A recognized expert on Chinese law, in 1974 he served on the National Council for U.S.-China Trade. 
9. Jon M. Huntsman, Jr., 2009 to 2011 
Twice elected as governor of Utah, Huntsman was tapped by President Barack Obama to serve as ambassador. He had previously lived in Asia three times and speaks fluent Mandarin. 
10. Gary F. Locke, 2011 to 2014 
Before arriving in China, Locke served as the secretary of commerce. As the administration's point person for achieving the president's National Export Initiative, he presided over a 17 percent increase in exports from 2009 to 2010, while exports to China saw a 32 percent increase. 
11. Max Sieben Baucus, 2014 to present 
Baucus, a former senator for Montana, has extensive experience in international trade. He was deeply involved in orchestrating congressional approval of permanent normal trade relations with China in 2000 and in facilitating China's entrance into the World Trade Organization in 2001.
